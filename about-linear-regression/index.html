Active Learning

If we can only measure for limited extra times, for example, 5 times more, then how could we best utilize them?

We need to measure the value that we are least sure about, which is the value with the maximal variance.

  1. Form predictive distribution p(y0∣x0,y,X)forallunmeasuredp(y_0|x_0, y, X) for all unmeasuredp(y0​∣x0​,y,X)forallunmeasuredx_0 \in D$
  2. Pick the x0x_0x0​ for which σ02\sigma_0^2σ02​ is largest and measure y0y_0y0​
  3. Update the posterior p(w∣y;X)p(w|y; X)p(w∣y;X) where y←(y;y0)y\leftarrow(y; y_0)y←(y;y0​) and X←(X;x0)X \leftarrow (X; x_0)X←(X;x0​)
  4. Return to #1 using the updated posterior

And for LR, it is maximizing x0TΣx0x_0^T\Sigma x_0x0T​Σx0​.

In fact, this process is minimizing the entropy, which is

H(p)=−∫p(z)ln⁡p(z)dzH(p)=-\int p(z)\ln p(z)dzH(p)=−∫p(z)lnp(z)dz

And the entropy for multivariate Gaussian is:

H(N(w∣μ,Σ))=12ln⁡((2πe)d∣Σ∣)H(N(w|\mu, \Sigma))=\frac{1}{2}\ln((2\pi e)^d|\Sigma|)H(N(w∣μ,Σ))=21​ln((2πe)d∣Σ∣)

And adding x0x_0x0​ would make the variance increase by

(λI+σ−2XTX)−1≡Σ⇒(λI+σ−2(x0x0TXTX))−1≡(Σ−1+σ−2x0x0T)−1(\lambda I+\sigma^{-2}X^TX)^{-1}\equiv\Sigma \Rightarrow (\lambda I+\sigma^{-2}(x_0x_0^TX^TX))^{-1}\equiv(\Sigma^{-1}+\sigma^{-2}x_0x_0^T)^{-1}(λI+σ−2XTX)−1≡Σ⇒(λI+σ−2(x0​x0T​XTX))−1≡(Σ−1+σ−2x0​x0T​)−1

And the change in entropy is:

Hpost=Hprior−d2ln⁡(1+σ−2x0TΣx0)H_{post}=H_{prior}-\frac{d}{2}\ln(1+\sigma^{-2}x_0^T\Sigma x_0)Hpost​=Hprior​−2d​ln(1+σ−2x0T​Σx0​)

Hence, this method is a greedy algorithm to minimize entropy.

LASSO

LS and RR are not suited for high-dimensional data, because:

  • They weight all dimensions without favoring subsets of dimensions.
  • The unknown “important” dimensions are mixed in with irrelevant ones.
  • They generalize poorly to new data, weights may not be interpretable.
  • LS solution not unique when d > n, meaning no unique predictions.

One intuition why RR is not good for high dimensional data is that the quadratic penalty will reduce more when reducing the larger terms, and will leave all terms of www small but not 0.

And to find a sparse solution (solution with many 0s), we need to use a linear penalty term. And that is why we introduce LASSO.

Model definition

wlasso=argmin⁡w∣∣y−Xw∣∣22+λ∣∣w∣∣1w_{lasso}=arg\min_w||y-Xw||^2_2+\lambda||w||_1wlasso​=argwmin​∣∣y−Xw∣∣22​+λ∣∣w∣∣1​

where ∣∣w∣∣1=∑i=1d∣wj∣||w||_1=\sum_{i=1}^d|w_j|∣∣w∣∣1​=∑i=1d​∣wj​∣.

With this penalty, the cost reduction does not depend on the magnitude of wjw_jwj​, which helps to get a sparse solution.

From the above figure, we could observe that with the increasing of λ\lambdaλ, which is the decreasing of df(λ)df(\lambda)df(λ), the weight will goes to 0 and will stay at 0. (In some special case, they may leave 0, but it's rare).

And more generally speaking, there is lpl_plp​ regression, where p=1p=1p=1 is LASSO and p=2p=2p=2 is RR. And notice when p<1p<1p<1, the penalty is no longer convex and we cannot get an global optimum.
